Walk through any cannabis menu and one number does most of the talking: the THC percentage. It gets printed bigger than the strain name in some shops, and plenty of shoppers read it like a score out of 100. So let's answer the question properly: what does THC percentage mean — and how much should it actually drive what you order?

Short answer: it tells you how much THC the flower contains by weight. What it doesn't tell you is how the night will feel. A 28% jar is not automatically a better evening than a 21% jar, and the gap between the two is far smaller than the arithmetic suggests. Here's the whole picture, in plain English.

## What does THC percentage actually measure?

THC percentage is a content measurement: the share of the flower's dry weight that is total THC. A gram of 24% flower carries roughly 240 milligrams of THC. A 3.5g eighth at the same number carries about 840mg across the whole jar — which you then portion out across many sessions.

One wrinkle worth knowing: raw flower barely contains active THC at all. Most of it is stored as THCA, a compound that converts to THC when heated. That's why flower is smoked, vaporized, or baked into infusions rather than eaten raw, and why the label says "total THC" — it's an estimate of what the flower delivers after that heat conversion, not what's sitting in the jar at room temperature.

The closest everyday analogy is ABV on a bottle. The percentage tells you precisely how much alcohol is in the glass — and almost nothing about whether you're holding a delicate Riesling or a smoky Islay Scotch. Content is one variable. Character is an entirely different conversation.

## Why 28% doesn't feel 40% stronger than 20%

On paper, 28 is forty percent more than 20. In practice, nobody experiences flower as arithmetic. Three things bend the math:

- **Terpenes steer the experience.** Terpenes are the aromatic compounds that give each strain its smell and much of its character. A myrcene-heavy strain tends to read as couch-cozy and slow; a limonene-forward one reads brighter and more social. A terpene-rich 21% flower often lands more vividly than a flat, one-note 28% — which is why longtime flower people sniff the jar before they read the label.
- **Your tolerance sets the scale.** For an occasional consumer, the jump from 20% to 28% is real and noticeable. For a frequent consumer, it compresses toward a rounding error. The same number means different things to different bodies — and to the same body on different weeks.
- **The session matters more than the spec.** How much you load, how long the session runs, whether it's a quick pre-roll on a walk or a slow bowl on the couch — these swing the outcome more than eight percentage points ever will. A light session of 28% flower and a generous session of 20% can land in exactly the same place.

There's also simple loss: combustion burns off a meaningful share of THC before it ever reaches you. The label number is a ceiling, not a delivered amount.

## Is higher THC better?

Here's the honest answer to "is higher THC better": higher THC is just more THC. Whether that's better depends entirely on the evening you have planned.

Judging flower by percentage alone is like judging coffee by caffeine content — you'd rank gas-station drip above a perfect espresso. A well-grown, properly cured 22% with a loud terpene profile is a richer experience than a bone-dry 29% that smells like nothing. The number measures quantity; it says nothing about craft.

What's worth weighing alongside the percentage:

- **Fit.** An Indica or Indica-Hybrid lean suits a slow, wind-down evening; a Sativa lean suits social energy or a creative spark. Matching the lean to your plans matters more than out-scoring last week's jar.
- **Aroma.** The louder and more distinct the smell, the richer the terpene profile — and terpenes shape the character of the experience as much as THC shapes its weight.
- **Freshness and cure.** A sticky, springy, fragrant bud beats a dry one at any percentage. The number on the label can't tell you how the flower was handled after harvest.

Price is a separate axis too. THC% and price don't move in lockstep — pricing tracks genetics, brand, and cure quality more than the number on the label.

## A sensible way to choose a THC percentage

Start from how you want the evening to feel, then use the number to fine-tune — not the other way around. A rough map:

- **15–22%** — the easygoing range. A good home for newer or occasional consumers, low-key weeknights, and anyone who puts flavor first.
- **22–28%** — pronounced. Experienced consumers will feel it clearly, and a little goes further.
- **28%+** — the top of the menu. These reward an established tolerance and modest sessions; for newcomers, a lower number is the smoother on-ramp.

If you'd rather not think in percentages at all, the [strain finder quiz](/quiz) asks how you want to feel and returns three matched picks from the live menu in about 30 seconds.

Recommendations here are educational, not medical advice. Cannabis affects each person differently — start low, go slow, and give every session time to land before adding more.

## Common questions

**Is a 30% flower twice as strong as a 15% one?**
By weight, it carries twice the THC. As an experience, no — terpenes, tolerance, and session size all bend the curve. Most people simply consume less of a higher-percentage flower and arrive at a similar place.

**What's a reasonable THC percentage for a first-timer?**
The 15–22% range with a Hybrid lean is the classic starting point. Keep the first session small — it's easy to add more later and impossible to subtract.

**Why does the same strain show a different THC% from batch to batch?**
Because cannabis is an agricultural product. Light, nutrients, harvest timing, and cure all shift the final number, so a few points of drift between harvests of the same strain is completely normal.

**Do edibles have a THC percentage?**
No — [edibles](/menu/edibles) are measured in milligrams per piece, with 5mg and 10mg gummies as the common formats. Milligrams are the more exact system: a flower percentage is an estimate of content, while an edible dose is a measured serving.
